Viewpoint: How Climate Change Is Creating Uninsurable Areas Across Europe

Source: www.insurancejournal.com · Score: 0.80
Why it matters: Discusses climate-driven insurance market gaps and risk-transfer tools (CAT bonds, parametric insurance) and public-private schemes relevant to Lloyd's market.
  • Climate change is creating 'uninsurable areas' as insurers withdraw or price out coverage — exemplified by State Farm nonrenewing ~72,000 California policies and the FAIR Plan growing from ~271,000 policies (2022) to >684,000 (Mar 2026), nearly collapsing after the Jan 2025 LA wildfires and requiring a $1bn emergency bailout.
  • The protection gap is already large and worsening: Swiss Re estimates 57% of global natural catastrophe losses went uninsured in 2024; historically ~75% in Europe. Market strain is showing in rising premiums (Germany warnings of potential doubling) and stresses on public schemes (France’s CatNat running deficits; surcharge raised from 12% to 20% in Jan 2025).
  • Market-based solutions can help but have limits: catastrophe (CAT) bonds shift extreme risk to capital markets and lock in multi‑year capacity, while parametric products enable rapid, model‑triggered pay-outs without loss adjustment — useful in low‑penetration or remote markets.
  • Government and public‑private responses are scaling up but face funding and transition challenges: examples include the UK Flood Re (set to expire 2039), France’s compulsory CatNat, and an EU reinsurance proposal that modelling suggests could cut Europe’s protection gap to ~10% but would require up to €65bn of public backstop capacity.
