Re/insurers seek more advanced PVT risk models for data centres: Synthetik’s Tim Brewer - Reinsurance News

Source: www.reinsurancene.ws · Score: 0.85
Why it matters: Insurers/reinsurers' modelling of data-centre PVT risks and portfolio accumulation—topic for Lloyd's market news.
  • Data centres are specialised, high-value exposures with significant tail risk — internal equipment is far more sensitive than typical contents and value concentrated per site due to heavy networked dependencies (power, cooling, fibre).
  • PVT threat profiles are region-dependent (e.g., drone/missile strikes in conflict zones versus strikes, riots and civil commotion elsewhere) and interact with structure and dependencies, often decoupling visible building damage from internal equipment loss and business interruption timelines.
  • Current models can triage risk but insurers/reinsurers need more advanced, scenario- and physics-informed modelling to quantify losses by hazard type, capture non-linear tail risks, and translate relative risk into monetary loss estimates.
  • Recommended approach for accumulation management: assess exposures through four lenses — physical/building characteristics; critical dependencies (transmission, fibre, cooling, water); service/customer mix (single hyperscaler vs mixed clients); and contractual/insurance terms — while adopting consistent taxonomy and portfolio-level differentiation.

State high court rejects insurers’ coverage denial in sex trafficking case - Business Insurance

Source: www.businessinsurance.com · Score: 0.72
Why it matters: PA Supreme Court ruling on insurer duty to defend/indemnify in sex-trafficking case; relevant to policy coverage and underwriting.
  • Pennsylvania Supreme Court unanimously ruled in Samsung Fire & Harleysville v. RI Settlement Trust that insurers cannot invoke a vague “public policy” defense to deny the contractual duty to defend or indemnify claims arising from alleged sex trafficking at a hotel.
  • The dispute arose from lawsuits by women trafficked as minors at the Roosevelt Inn; insurers sought to avoid coverage citing Pennsylvania’s human trafficking law and a 2004 plurality decision involving a heroin seller, but the high court rejected that reasoning and said the 2004 plurality is not controlling.
  • Court emphasized that insurers must draft express policy exclusions if they intend to exclude coverage for criminal conduct or abuse of minors; a concurrence noted readily available exclusions exist and criticized using public policy to override clear contract terms.
  • Practical implications for insurers and brokers: expect exposure to defense and likely indemnity obligations in hotel trafficking suits unless specific exclusions are present; review policy language, consider targeted exclusions and underwriting scrutiny for hospitality risks, and anticipate heightened claims/reserve activity given recent multi‑million dollar verdicts and the federal statute permitting suits against entities that “benefit” from trafficking.
