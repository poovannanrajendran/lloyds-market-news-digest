What is the ‘biggest constraint’ in the captive model?

Source: www.insurancetimes.co.uk · Score: 0.75
Why it matters: Covers captives, fronting and multinational risk programmes—topics impacting Lloyd's market dynamics.
  • Captives are gaining traction among large multinationals as a risk-management tool, but geography/licensing remains the biggest constraint — companies often still need local policies in jurisdictions their captive can’t cover.
  • Two primary captive structures exist: direct pricing captives (require an insurance licence, more capital and internal actuarial/underwriting/support functions, domicile-dependent) and reinsurance captives (insurers place local policies and the captive reinsures some of that risk).
  • Fronting insurers remain essential for regulatory compliance and local placements; captives currently supplement rather than replace the insurance market where local licensing is required.
  • Insurers that want to support growing captive programmes should provide flexibility, a broad international network aligned to client footprints, embedded efficient processes and technology to streamline administration of multinational programmes.

Colorado bill would allow employers to revise experience mods after claim closures - Business Insurance

Source: www.businessinsurance.com · Score: 0.65
Why it matters: State-level workers' comp reform affecting experience mods and premiums; impacts underwriting/pricing relevant to Lloyd's market.
  • Colorado S.B. 175 would let employers (or licensed producers) request revisions to workers’ compensation experience modification factors (e-mods) when a closed claim’s actual paid amount is lower than reserves used while the claim was open.
  • Insurers would be required to update the e-mod to reflect the final paid amount (not the higher reserve) if the adjustment would reduce the e-mod by at least 0.05 or bring it to 1.0 or below; insurers must then instruct the rating organization to revise the e-mod.
  • Timing and administrative deadlines: employers must notify the insurer between initial claim reporting and up to 31 days after the employer’s rating effective date; rating organizations would have 30 days to complete the adjustment.
  • Insurers would be required to credit employers for any premium changes resulting from the adjustment during the policy period; the bill would take effect Jan. 1, 2027 and apply to claims closed on or after that date.
