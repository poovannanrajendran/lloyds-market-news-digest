Insurers Avoid €580 Million Hit From Nord Stream Pipeline Blasts

Source: www.insurancejournal.com · Score: 0.80
Why it matters: Mentions Lloyd's Insurance Company SA and a London court ruling on war exclusion in the Nord Stream sabotage case.
  • London court ruled insurers (Lloyd’s Insurance Co SA and Arch Insurance (EU) DAC) are not liable for €580m claim by Nord Stream AG because the pipeline damage fell within the policies’ war exclusion (Exclusion 2.i).
  • Judge Clare Moulder found the damage was “directly or indirectly occasioned by, happening through, or in consequence of war,” applying a causation standard that does not require pinpointing the exact perpetrator.
  • Although criminal probes and expert evidence discussed likely perpetrators (including Ukrainian divers), the court made no factual finding on who carried out the sabotage and held that identifying the perpetrator was unnecessary to trigger the war exclusion.
  • Practical implications for insurers and underwriters: reinforces broad application of war/excluded-peril wording to sabotage in an armed-conflict context; highlights importance of precise drafting of war and government-action clauses and careful assessment of causation and factual matrices in major politically sensitive claims.

Texas court says farm liability law shields nonsubscriber ranch employer - Business Insurance

Source: www.businessinsurance.com · Score: 0.70
Why it matters: Texas Farm Animal Liability Act ruling affects farm liability coverage for nonsubscriber farms; implications for underwriting and ag-risk.
  • Texas Court of Appeals (11th Dist.) held in Kinnibrugh v. Poteet that the Farm Animal Liability Act (FALA) shields a ranch employer (a nonsubscriber to workers’ compensation) from a wrongful‑death suit where a ranch hand was killed by a hydraulic cattle squeeze chute during cattle vaccination, finding FALA covers risks inherent in farm animal activities even when an animal did not directly cause the injury.
  • The court concluded vaccinating cattle is expressly covered and the chute’s use was directly related to that activity, reversing a trial court denial of summary judgment and rejecting an open‑courts constitutional challenge by treating the claim as statutory rather than a protected common‑law claim.
  • Although FALA has exceptions permitting recovery in some situations, the appeals court found none applied here: despite the chute creating an “extreme risk,” there was insufficient evidence the ranch owners were subjectively aware that the worker was in danger when the chute was released (a subjective awareness standard).
  • Practical implications for insurers: potential reduced third‑party liability exposure for agricultural employers under FALA could influence underwriting and pricing for farm/ranch accounts, but outcomes will turn on case facts (especially subjective knowledge), and carriers should monitor appeals, similar cases, and any legislative responses.
