Briefing: Adjuster’s plea may leave marine sector all at sea

Source: www.insurancetimes.co.uk · Score: 0.80
Why it matters: Mentions Lloyd's-hosted AAA conference and potential reforms to marine hull insurance impacting the Lloyd's market.
  • At the AAA annual conference (7 May 2026) outgoing chair Chris Kilbee urged wholesale reform of the UK 1983 Institute Time Clauses – Hulls (ITC), saying the clauses are aging, misaligned with modern commercial realities and contribute to inconsistencies and inefficiencies in claims handling where time is now as critical as cost.
  • The marine hull market still leans on principles dating from the 1890s (York–Antwerp/General Average) and the 1983 ITC; General Average (GA) remains the mechanism to share voyage losses proportionally but was historically focused on cargo rather than crew welfare.
  • Protection & indemnity (P&I) clubs never signed up to GA rules and have been reluctant to issue GA guarantees for cargo interests; despite changes in priorities (greater emphasis on crew), P&I resistance persists and complicates attempts to modernise GA arrangements.
  • Any revision of hull wording or GA practices would revive contentious debates with material implications for underwriting, claims allocation, P&I involvement and operational processes—market participants should prepare for lively discussion and potential contractual and claims-handling change.

Biba 2026: Brokers and insurers face new liability questions over self driving cars

Source: www.insurancetimes.co.uk · Score: 0.78
Why it matters: Covers liability attribution and underwriting implications of autonomous vehicles in motor insurance, a key Lloyd's market topic.
  • Autonomous vehicles shift risk away from traditional driver-behaviour factors toward product-adjacent risks — insurers must assess software stacks, hardware performance and AI capabilities alongside usual underwriting data.
  • Fault attribution is increasingly complex in shared-control scenarios, raising uncertainty over whether liability lies with the human driver, software developer or vehicle manufacturer.
  • Under UK compulsory motor rules insurers will still typically pay third-party claims first but retain rights of recovery against manufacturers or software providers, affecting claims handling and subrogation strategies.
  • Brokers and specialist underwriters will play a larger advisory role; insurers need new products, data sources and technical expertise to underwrite autonomous-vehicle and supplier/manufacturer liabilities effectively.
