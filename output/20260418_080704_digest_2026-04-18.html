Wis. high court upholds asbestos verdict against Pabst - Business Insurance

Source: www.businessinsurance.com · Score: 0.75
Why it matters: Covers insurance risk, liability, and workers' comp issues relevant to Lloyd's underwriting and risk assessment.
  • Wisconsin Supreme Court held that the estate of a steamfitter can pursue a negligence claim under the state’s safe‑place statute against Pabst for asbestos exposure, finding the statute can apply to “frequenters” such as contractor employees.
  • Court found sufficient evidence that Pabst retained control over work areas and failed to address known asbestos hazards (extensive asbestos‑insulated piping, chipping that released dust, continued use of asbestos, failures to warn and later OSHA citation), so liability and submission of punitive damages to the jury were upheld.
  • The court reduced punitive damages under Wisconsin law: punitive awards are capped at twice the compensatory damages the plaintiff can actually recover from that defendant, so the jury’s $20M punitive award was limited to about $4.66M based on Pabst’s ~$2.33M recoverable compensatory share.
  • Implications for insurers: premises owners face expanded third‑party exposure when contractor employees qualify as frequenters; allocation of compensatory liability among defendants affects per‑defendant punitive caps and insurer exposure; review of indemnity/insured‑contract provisions, punitive‑damages coverage positions, and asbestos abatement and contractor control practices is advisable.

Source: www.businessinsurance.com · Score: 0.72
Why it matters: Discusses workers' comp coverage and legal standards for traveling employees, with implications for insurers.
  • Illinois appellate court reversed an IWCC decision and remanded a Subway employee death-benefits award, finding the commission applied the wrong legal standard in a 2022 delivery-driver fatal crash case.
  • Legal takeaway: claims by "traveling employees" must be judged under a "reasonable and foreseeable" conduct standard, not the more lenient "intentional or reckless misconduct" test the commission used.
  • Case facts relevant to compensability include speeding, marijuana in the employee's system, and possible cell-phone distraction — conduct that may be deemed unreasonable/unforeseeable and defeat coverage on remand.
  • Implications for insurers: reassess handling and defense strategies for traveling-worker claims; review underwriting, policy language and exclusions; and bolster loss-control measures (drug testing, vehicle-use policies, driver training) for employees who travel as part of work.
