How Niche Insurance Shielded Bad Bunny From Bad Weather

Source: www.insurancejournal.com · Score: 0.80
Why it matters: Describes parametric weather insurance and on-site sensors for live events, a core Lloyd's market risk solution.
  • Temporary on-site weather stations can unlock parametric cover for last-minute outdoor events by eliminating basis risk when official sensors are too distant — as with the Bad Bunny Medellín concerts where a bespoke rainfall-triggered policy was deployed days before the shows.
  • Parametric structures provide rapid, proof-free payouts tied to measurable triggers (rainfall thresholds in this case) and can be scaled to large limits (Descartes cited capacity up to $80M), making them attractive for concerts, sports and other weather-sensitive live events.

  • Better, hyper-local data reduces underwriting uncertainty and basis risk, enabling more accurate pricing and potentially lower premiums, but the very short time windows for events increase atmospheric volatility and complicate risk modeling.

Conn. court backs employer lien on full asbestos settlements - Business Insurance

Source: www.businessinsurance.com · Score: 0.72
Why it matters: CT court ruling on workers' comp lien extends to full asbestos settlement proceeds, impacting coverage and lien practices.
  • Connecticut Supreme Court (Dodge v. Commissioner of Motor Vehicles) held that state and municipal employers can assert a workers’ compensation lien on the full amount of third‑party asbestos settlements tied to an employee’s occupational disease, even where much of the recovery reflects non‑work exposures.
  • Case facts: the decedent suffered mesothelioma from both occupational and non‑occupational asbestos exposure; his estate obtained >$500,000 in product‑liability settlements and the employee had received workers’ compensation benefits after a finding that occupational exposure was a substantial contributing factor.
  • Legal rationale: the court treated mesothelioma as a single compensable occupational disease with multiple causes; because the disease was substantially linked to employment, the employers’ lien rights mirror their obligation to pay benefits and therefore extend to all settlement proceeds attributable to that injury, regardless of non‑work exposure.
  • Practical implications for insurers and employers: expect broader employer recovery/subrogation claims on third‑party settlements, increased allocation disputes, and potential impacts on reserves, settlement strategy and claims handling — insurers should reassess subrogation, reimbursement practices and defense/allocation positions in Connecticut (and consider implications for similar jurisdictions).
