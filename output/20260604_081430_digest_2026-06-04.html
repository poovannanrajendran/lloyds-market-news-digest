Heat exposure complicates causation in workers comp claims - Business Insurance

Source: www.businessinsurance.com · Score: 0.80
Why it matters: Discusses heat exposure affecting causation in workers' comp claims, a key insurance market risk and claims topic.
  • Heat is increasingly a complicating factor in workers’ comp: claims often aren’t classic heat illness but whether heat materially contributed to fainting, falls, cardiac symptoms, dehydration or other events. Adjusters must assess heat as a possible contributing cause, not just a primary diagnosis.
  • Compensability still turns on course-and-scope and whether job conditions materially contributed; incidents off the job (e.g., commutes) are generally not compensable. Preexisting conditions, medications and off-duty activity don’t automatically bar coverage but complicate causation analyses.
  • Effective adjudication requires expanded evidence collection beyond routine FNOL: medical records, witness statements, weather and indoor-temperature data, job descriptions, PPE, hydration/break/acclimatization records, and medication history. Immediate medical response remains the first priority; compensability is reviewed afterward.
  • Operational actions for insurers: update FNOL and claims intake to capture heat/weather data, train examiners to recognize heat contribution, enhance documentation standards, and monitor subrogation pathways from group health where work-related heat exposure may surface.
