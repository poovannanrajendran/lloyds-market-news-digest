The architectural decision every specialty acquirer is making implicitly

Source: www.globalreinsurance.com · Score: 0.92
Why it matters: Discusses Lloyd’s market M&A, platform/integration architecture, and CDR readiness implications.
  • P&C acquirers routinely push technology into a Phase‑3 integration workstream, which implicitly hands the architectural decision to the surviving P&C platform — a mismatch for specialty books that is expensive and slow to reverse (often ~3 years).
  • P&C platforms (Guidewire, Duck Creek, etc.) are designed for standardized, volume P&C; specialty business requires negotiated, broker-driven placement, multi‑underwriter line structures and Lloyd’s‑specific data fields, so running specialty on P&C systems produces bolt‑ons, workflow gaps and commercial friction.
  • The Lloyd’s Blueprint Two sunset and delayed central replatforming mean many managing agents are building their own CDR‑compatible architectures; forcing specialty onto a P&C platform can create an unscoped regulatory remediation (capture‑at‑source/C﻿DR) risk that must be identified early.
  • Integration sponsors must front‑load the architectural decision in the first 90 days: explicitly declare which platform will govern (and why), assess the acquired book’s CDR/capture‑at‑source readiness against that platform, and assign clear accountability and authority for the architecture choice rather than deferring it to a later technology workstream.

Florida court denies comp immunity for contractor in fatal fall case - Business Insurance

Source: www.businessinsurance.com · Score: 0.68
Why it matters: Legal ruling on workers' compensation immunity impacts liability exposure and insurance risk in construction projects.
  • Florida 2nd District Court of Appeal affirmed that Willis A. Smith Construction was not entitled to workers’ compensation immunity in the wrongful death suit of subcontractor part‑owner Phillip Scott Keathley, who fell from a fourth‑floor balcony during a university restoration project (Willis A. Smith Construction Inc. v. Keathley, Apr. 28, 2026).
  • Court’s holding: the general contractor was not the statutory employer because there was no subcontracting relationship—West Shore Construction only received an invitation to bid, never submitted a bid, was never hired, performed no contracted work, and there was no contractual privity, compensation or delegation.
  • Legal/practical takeaway: Florida statutory‑employer immunity (and the related bar on tort claims) requires actual subcontracting/contractual delegation—mere bidding contacts or anticipated work do not confer immunity. Contractors remain at tort exposure unless the statutory criteria are demonstrably met.
  • Insurance implications: increased potential liability for general contractors in similar fact patterns; carriers should scrutinize contractual relationships, indemnity clauses, certificates of insurance, and proof of subcontract execution when underwriting, pricing, and defending claims.
