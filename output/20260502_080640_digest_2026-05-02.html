Howden Re: Baltimore bridge loss resets marine reinsurance benchmark

Source: www.globalreinsurance.com · Score: 0.86
Why it matters: Discusses reinsurance capacity and pricing implications for the Lloyd's market following a major marine loss.
  • $2.8bn insured loss from the Francis Scott Key Bridge collapse is now the largest marine insurance loss on record — driven mainly by bridge replacement (settlement framework with Maryland ~ $2.5bn) and supplemented by pollution, wreck removal and lost toll revenues.
  • Losses will be concentrated in the International Group P&I reinsurance/retrocession layers — pooling plus excess-of-loss programmes mean the deepest hit falls on large reinsurers and retrocessionaires, representing a material single-loss for some balance sheets.
  • The event compounds other recent stresses (aviation leasing settlements, Middle East-related claims) but must be viewed within broader portfolios that also include large nat-cat exposures; aggregate strain is meaningful even if a $2.8bn marine loss is small relative to extreme nat-cat scenarios.
  • Capital remains abundant and new entrants have maintained competitive conditions (pricing softened at April 2026 renewals), but this event will test pricing discipline and is likely to push up marine liability rates over time; insurers and buyers should proactively reassess appetite and positioning ahead of a potential market turn.

Source: www.reinsurancene.ws · Score: 0.82

  • Insured loss now reported in excess of $2.8bn (up from early $1.5bn working assumptions), making Baltimore the largest single marine insurance loss on record and reflecting a ~$1.3bn deterioration that surprised the market.
  • The bulk of the loss will be borne by major reinsurers and the retrocession market via the International Group of P&I Clubs’ pooling and excess‑of‑loss programme; for some reinsurers this represents a very significant single‑event hit relative to capital.
  • Principal drivers of the increase are the bridge replacement settlement (c. $2.5bn paid to Chubb) plus pollution liabilities, wreck removal and lost toll revenues, with legal, salvage and reconstruction costs continuing to evolve.
  • Market implications include upward pressure on marine liability rates, but an ambiguous broader market response: excess capacity and new capital that drove 15–20% softening in parts of the April 2026 renewals may blunt a rapid correction, so carriers should reassess appetite and proactively position portfolios.

Willis introduces Merger Protect to address US antitrust review costs in M&A - Reinsurance News

Source: www.reinsurancene.ws · Score: 0.80
Why it matters: New M&A regulatory cost-insurance product (Merger Protect) by Willis; relevant to insurance market developments and risk transfer.
  • Willis has launched Merger Protect to insure against the financial impact of US antitrust regulatory reviews in M&A, specifically covering costs arising from Hart‑Scott‑Rodino Second Requests and related enforcement activity; target clients include buyers, sellers, sponsors and their advisers.
  • Policy reimburses specified response costs (subject to agreed retentions and limits) such as external legal fees, economists and industry specialists, consultants, e‑discovery, data collection/hosting, document review/production and witness preparation; cover can continue into enforcement where relevant.
  • Typically arranged early in a reportable transaction before any regulatory request, the product aims to convert uncertain, potentially large regulatory response costs into a defined insurance expense to protect deal value and reduce operational disruption.
  • Willis’ Litigation and Contingent Risk Solutions team tailors cover to transaction size, sector and regulatory exposure, using Second Request and enforcement trend data to structure terms and limits.
