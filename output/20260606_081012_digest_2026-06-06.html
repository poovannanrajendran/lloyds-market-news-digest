Earthquake models fail to factor in events driving two-thirds of recent industry losses: MS Amlin - Artemis.bm

Source: www.artemis.bm · Score: 0.90
Why it matters: Supershear earthquake risk is underrepresented in models, impacting Lloyd's market re/insurance, cat bonds, and ILS pricing/capital.
  • Supershear earthquakes — ruptures that travel faster than usual producing a “sonic‑boom” and double‑pulse shaking — are absent from many hazard and catastrophe models yet were linked to 66% of insured earthquake losses since 2016 (~US$13.2bn).
  • MS Amlin warns models may understate earthquake risk by as much as 60% in extreme cases; their portfolio tests show 200‑year losses rising ~5–10% and 500‑year losses increasing ~30–60% when supershear effects are included.
  • Material market impact for insurance, reinsurance and ILS/cat bond investors: potential underpricing and under‑capitalisation across quake exposures (notably California), with ~US$6bn of pure US earthquake cat bonds and a portion of ~US$17.6bn of US multi‑peril cat bonds at risk.
  • Action items for underwriting and portfolio teams: urgently incorporate supershear scenarios into catastrophe models, stress tests, pricing, parametric triggers and capital planning while model vendors update US earthquake models — a narrow window exists to close this blind spot before the next major event.

Appeals court says Taco Bell worker’s fatal stabbing limited to comp - Business Insurance

Source: www.businessinsurance.com · Score: 0.68
Why it matters: Centers on workers' compensation exclusivity and a premises liability claim affecting insurance coverage and risk management.
  • California Court of Appeal (Third Appellate District) affirmed dismissal of a wrongful death/survival action in Wrenne v. JA Sutherland Inc., holding workers' compensation is the exclusive remedy after a Taco Bell crew member was fatally stabbed in the restaurant parking lot during a 30‑minute unpaid meal break.
  • Court applied the "premises line rule," finding the injury arose in the course and scope of employment because the employee was on the employer's premises during an authorized break; unpaid status did not preclude comp exclusivity.
  • The court rejected the family's argument that the act was purely personal and concluded the stabbing would not have occurred but for the employee's presence at work, and affirmed dismissal without leave to amend as plaintiffs could not plead a viable civil claim.
  • Implications for insurers and risk managers: on‑premises incidents during authorized breaks may be confined to workers' compensation cover, affecting third‑party liability exposure, reserves, underwriting, security risk assessments and defense strategies.

  • Worker injured left knee at work (Aug. 15, 2023), underwent an authorized knee replacement, then reported post‑op leg pain; vascular testing showed severe great saphenous vein reflux that a specialist said likely preexisted the injury and that the surgery made symptoms more apparent but was not the direct cause.
  • Tennessee workers’ compensation judge denied authorization for treatment of venous insufficiency because the claimant failed to show the work injury and subsequent surgery were more than 50% responsible for aggravating the condition, as required under Tennessee law for compensable aggravation.
  • The court found the medical proof insufficient on both key causation/apportionment questions and concluded the claimant was unlikely to prevail at trial, so additional vascular treatment was denied “at this time”; the matter remains set for further proceedings.
  • Practical takeaways for insurers: obtain clear apportionment opinions addressing whether a work event contributed >50% to aggravation and need for treatment, document preexisting conditions thoroughly, and consider the evidentiary risk before authorizing post‑op specialist care for conditions with long‑standing pathology.
