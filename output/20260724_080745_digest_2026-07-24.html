Comp exclusivity bars worker’s assault, negligence claims - Business Insurance

Source: www.businessinsurance.com · Score: 0.72
Why it matters: Texas workers’ comp exclusivity bars assault/negligence claims; impacts employer coverage and workers’ comp risk.
  • Texas 14th Court of Appeals affirmed that the Texas Workers’ Compensation Act barred an employee’s assault and negligence claims after a workplace fight because the employee was undisputedly an employee and the employer was a WC subscriber, making workers’ comp the exclusive remedy.
  • The court rejected the intentional-tort exception for the assault claim, finding evidence that the crew leader lacked hiring, firing or disciplinary authority and therefore was not a ‘‘vice principal’’ whose intentional conduct could be imputed to the employer.
  • A retaliation claim under Chapter 21 of the Texas Labor Code failed because reporting the fight and participating in the company investigation did not constitute protected activity under the Texas Human Rights Act—the dispute related to protective equipment, not discrimination.
  • Implications for insurers/employers: workers’ comp exclusivity provides strong defense in worksite assaults absent vice-principal attribution or protected activity; maintain clear records of supervisory authority, discipline policies, investigations and any discrimination-related complaints to preserve WC defenses.

Canada's regulator adds catastrophe bonds as a form of reinsurance for capital credit - Artemis.bm

Source: www.artemis.bm · Score: 0.70
Why it matters: Canada OSFI expands capital relief for catastrophe bonds, impacting ILS and reinsurance strategies with potential implications for Lloyd's market participants.
  • OSFI has amended Section 4.3 of the Minimum Capital Test Guideline to allow natural catastrophe bonds to be recognised as reinsurance for insurance risk capital credit; the change is effective immediately and insurers must obtain OSFI approval before using cat bonds to reduce capital requirements.
  • Regulatory conditions: cat bond structures are treated as “unregistered reinsurance, with no margin requirement” and must have an indemnity trigger, collateral invested in high‑quality assets, and collateral held in Canada and fully paid under a reinsurance security agreement — otherwise no capital credit will be granted.
  • Market impact: the move creates greater parity between ILS and traditional reinsurance, is likely to encourage more Canadian catastrophe‑bond sponsorship and issuance, and provides insurers access to diversified reinsurance capital while expanding investor opportunities in Canada‑peril deals.
  • Precedent deals: TD Insurance sponsored the C$150m MMIFS Re Ltd. (Series 2025‑1) — the first Canada‑only natural cat bond — and returned in 2026 with a further C$115m MMIFS Re Ltd. (Series 2026‑1) issuance.
