In Focus: What’s going on with Blueprint Two after ‘quietly shelved’ report?

Source: www.insurancetimes.co.uk · Score: 0.92
Why it matters: Covers Lloyd's Blueprint Two status, delays and potential shelving—core market news.
  • Reports in Feb 2026 claimed Blueprint Two had been quietly shelved and the market engagement team disbanded; Lloyd’s disputes that characterisation and says it remains committed to re‑platforming to a resilient, cloud‑based infrastructure.
  • The programme has been repeatedly delayed since its 2020 launch: phase one was pushed into 2026 and Lloyd’s CEO says full re‑platforming is unlikely before 2028, with formal market testing not expected to start before 2026.
  • Market reaction is one of mounting frustration and disillusionment given the extended timelines, but industry bodies stress the programme’s scale (350 competitive firms) makes delivery inherently difficult.
  • The agreed near‑term approach is evolutionary not revolutionary: maintain heritage systems operationally resilient to at least 2030, have Velonetic continue infrastructure support, prioritise data standards (ACORD) and phased implementation of standard data requirements and functional exchanges.

Briefing: Is AI about to rewrite the rules of insurance distribution?

Source: www.insurancetimes.co.uk · Score: 0.75
Why it matters: Covers AI-driven insurance distribution and Consumer Duty implications in the UK market, affecting Lloyd's-style insurers and brokers.
  • Generative AI (e.g. ChatGPT) is entering insurance distribution—OpenAI, Experian and MoneySuperMarket now offer quote/compare apps that let customers start, refine and buy policies via a single conversational interface rather than broker sites or PCWs.
  • This conversational channel could replicate the structural effects of price comparison websites—aggregating demand, concentrating the customer relationship, compressing margins and shifting the competitive battleground upstream.
  • Consumer Duty risks are acute: fluent AI interactions can create a false impression of guidance and understanding, so firms must ensure policy scope, exclusions, limits and likely claims outcomes are clearly conveyed—not lost to simplicity or headline features.
  • Conversational AI is not intrinsically problematic: if well designed it can improve accessibility and clarity, but it also risks accelerating commoditisation and concentrating distribution power outside traditional insurers and intermediaries.
