Discipline and drawdown limits define ILS managers over full market cycles: Francois Divet - Artemis.bm

Source: www.artemis.bm · Score: 0.72
Why it matters: Covers ILS market dynamics and cat bonds relevant to risk transfer and capacity in the Lloyd's market.
  • Assess ILS managers over full market cycles—strong managers maintain discipline, limit drawdowns in severe loss years and avoid “reaching for yield” late in the cycle.
  • Catastrophe bonds complement traditional reinsurance: they remove counterparty credit risk (fully funded SPV collateral), typically offer multi‑year protection (often 3 years) and can strengthen an insurer’s negotiating position with reinsurers.
  • Diversification is essential—across perils, regions (and subregions), layers and trigger types—to reduce drawdowns, but it cannot eliminate systemic tail risk if extreme events hit multiple peak‑peril exposures simultaneously.
  • Portfolio construction: BNP Paribas AM targets a fund‑level expected loss of ~2–2.5%, enforces hard peril/geography limits, manages exposures carefully and typically holds bonds to maturity unless underlying risk changes materially.

Comp bar applies in teen worker’s fatal heavy equipment accident - Business Insurance

Source: www.businessinsurance.com · Score: 0.68
Why it matters: Legal ruling on workers' comp exclusivity affects employer liability risk and coverage; impacts underwriting and risk transfer in the Lloyd's market.
  • Georgia Court of Appeals affirmed dismissal of parents’ tort claims after a 16‑year‑old Terra Excavating employee was killed when a compactor rolled off an elevated pad, holding the Georgia Workers’ Compensation Act provides the exclusive remedy.
  • Court found the supervisor was a co‑employee (same employer) and the injury arose in the course of employment, so ordinary suits against co‑employees are barred under the Act.
  • The plaintiffs’ argument that an “affirmative act” by the supervisor would allow tort claims failed: record showed the supervisor instructed the teen to stay toward the center of the pad, was not in the immediate area, and did not affirmatively direct operation of the compactor.
  • Practical implications for insurers: reinforces comp exclusivity in similar facts but highlights need to document supervision, training, contractor/subcontractor relationships and any supervisory directives—since evidence of intentional or affirmatively wrongful acts can be dispositive for exception analysis and coverage exposure.
